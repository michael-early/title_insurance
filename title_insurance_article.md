# Title Insurance as a Refinance Friction: What the Evidence Says and Why It Matters

---

## Introduction

Title insurance is one of the least-examined large line items in American housing finance. In 2025, the industry generated $18.6 billion in direct premiums written. Its net loss ratio — the share of premiums paid out as claims — was 4.4 percent. Its expense ratio was 98.5 percent. The gap between those numbers is striking: for every dollar collected in premiums, less than five cents went to pay claims, while nearly all the rest covered operating costs, agent commissions, and profits. By any standard insurance comparison, that structure raises questions.

Those questions are not new. The Government Accountability Office flagged them in 2006 and 2007. The CFPB raised them in a 2024 request for information on mortgage closing costs. Treasury convened a roundtable on title insurance in 2024 and published a synthesis note calling for more independent research. NAIC issued a request for research in 2024 and published a detailed fifty-state regulatory survey in 2025. The policy conversation has been building, and the central empirical questions have not been answered convincingly.

This article argues that the most important of those unanswered questions is not whether title insurance is "worth it" in some global welfare sense. It is something more specific: **do required title-insurance costs meaningfully suppress mortgage refinancing when interest rates decline?** If they do, the implications extend well beyond closing-cost fairness. Title insurance becomes a friction that slows monetary-policy transmission to households, keeps monthly payments higher than necessary, and concentrates that burden on the borrowers who can least afford it. That is a different kind of policy problem, and it points toward a different set of solutions.

## What Title Insurance Is and How the Market Works

Title insurance protects against defects in a property's ownership record — errors in prior conveyances, undisclosed liens, fraud, forgery, or gaps in the chain of title not revealed by a public-record search. In U.S. mortgage finance there are two policies: a **lender's policy**, which protects the mortgage lender up to the outstanding loan balance, and an **owner's policy**, which protects the homebuyer for as long as they own the property.

The distinction matters enormously for market structure. Lender's title insurance is effectively mandatory: Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac require it for virtually all loans delivered into their programs, and the same is true for FHA and VA loans. Owner's coverage is optional, though heavily encouraged. Because lenders require lender's title insurance to originate a conforming mortgage, borrowers do not choose whether to buy it — they choose only from which provider. In practice, even that choice is largely made for them.

Title insurance is typically purchased through a settlement agent or title company, chosen from a list provided by the lender or real estate agent. GAO documented in 2006 and 2007 that consumers rarely shop independently for title services. Title agents market aggressively to referral sources — lenders, real estate brokers, and builders — rather than to end borrowers. Affiliated business arrangements allow these referral relationships to generate fees, sometimes disclosed only minimally. The result is a market where the party paying the premium (the borrower) has little effective influence over the price or provider.

State law governs title premium rates, but regulatory models vary substantially. In **promulgated-rate states** — currently Texas, Florida, and New Mexico — the state sets the allowable premium schedule, and all title insurers must charge the same rate. Texas published a revised basic rate schedule in 2019 and again in 2026; Florida maintains its own risk-rate and reissue-rate tables. At the other end, some states use file-and-use or prior-approval models that allow more pricing flexibility but still regulate form and content. Iowa operates an entirely different system: a state-administered, not-for-profit Title Guaranty program that charges a flat fee and provides both lender and owner coverage at costs far below private-market alternatives. Treasury's 2024 note cited Iowa explicitly as a structural alternative worth studying.

One feature of title insurance that has received insufficient attention in the policy debate is its **recurrence in refinancing**. When a homeowner refinances, a new lender's title policy must be purchased — the old policy covered only the original lender. Most states allow "reissue" or "refinance" discounts on subsequent policies, but those discounts are partial. In Texas, for example, a reissue credit applies when the prior policy was issued within ten years, but the borrower still pays a meaningful fraction of the full premium. In a state with a promulgated rate of roughly $1,400 for a $250,000 loan, the refinance premium after a reissue discount might be $700 to $900. That is not a trivial upfront cost when the borrower is weighing whether a rate reduction saves enough each month to justify refinancing at all.

## The Economics of the Industry

The industry's primary defense of its cost structure rests on what it calls the **prevention argument**: the low paid-loss ratio reflects not the unimportance of title risk, but the effectiveness of title search, examination, and curative work done before closing. Under this view, the real product is risk elimination, not risk transfer, and the expense ratio reflects the labor-intensive work of searching records, clearing liens, and resolving defects — none of which shows up as a paid claim.

This argument has some merit. Title examination does reduce the probability of post-closing disputes, and the integration of title work into the closing process is a genuine service. Treasury's 2024 synthesis was notably balanced on this point, acknowledging both the protection benefit and the pricing concern.

But the prevention argument does not resolve the empirical puzzle. If title insurance premiums primarily covered the cost of examination and prevention work, we would expect premiums to correlate with the complexity and riskiness of title searches — older properties, chain-of-title problems, jurisdictions with poor record quality. Instead, premiums are typically set as a percentage of property or loan value. A $500,000 home in a county with an impeccable recording system pays twice the premium of a $250,000 home with an identical title history. That pricing structure is hard to reconcile with a pure prevention-cost story.

The HUD-sponsored research by Susan Woodward found that title charges in her FHA sample averaged around $1,200 per loan and varied with loan size in ways inconsistent with underlying production cost. The Urban Institute's five-market study similarly found large unexplained variation in title charges across and within counties, even after controlling for borrower characteristics, property features, and settlement-agent identity. Both studies concluded that the market is not functioning competitively in the standard sense: prices are not being set by consumers shopping against each other.

None of this means title insurance is worthless. The more precise conclusion is that **some protection at some price is valuable, but the market is likely organized and priced inefficiently**. That is the framing Treasury adopted in 2024, and it is the framing most consistent with the evidence.

## Title Insurance as a Refinance Friction

The economics of refinancing are sensitive to upfront costs in a way that purchase economics are not. A homeowner considering a refinance must weigh the one-time cost of refinancing — appraisal, origination fee, title insurance, other closing costs — against the expected monthly savings from a lower rate, discounted over the expected time remaining in the home. When upfront costs are higher, the threshold rate improvement needed to justify refinancing rises. Some households that would benefit from refinancing will not do so because the upfront hurdle is too high relative to the savings.

This is not a theoretical conjecture. Berger, Milbradt, Tourre, and Vavra showed rigorously that upfront refinance costs produce meaningful refinance inaction in mortgage markets, and that the distribution of that inaction is not random — it is concentrated among households for whom the cost-to-savings ratio is least favorable. Because title insurance is a required, recurring cost every time a mortgage is refinanced, any policy or geographic variation in title costs should translate into variation in refinance responsiveness.

The empirical implication is testable. States differ in their title premium schedules. A homeowner in Texas with a $250,000 mortgage faces a promulgated-rate lender's title premium of roughly $1,200–1,400, reduced to perhaps $700–900 with a reissue discount on refinance. A homeowner across the state line in Iowa may pay a flat fee of a few hundred dollars under the Iowa Title Guaranty program. When national mortgage rates decline and the financial incentive to refinance improves, we would expect refinancing to respond more strongly in Iowa than in Texas — not because Iowa homeowners are more financially sophisticated, but because the upfront hurdle is lower.

The cleanest empirical design to test this would compare **refinance versus purchase mortgage outcomes** across **neighboring counties on either side of state borders**, as national mortgage-rate declines shift the incentive to refinance over time. This "triple-difference" structure is useful because: (1) national rate changes are plausibly unrelated to state title-cost regimes; (2) border counties share much of the same local economy, housing market, and demographic composition; and (3) many local shocks that affect both purchase and refinance lending cancel out in the within-county purchase-versus-refinance comparison, while the title cost friction should load disproportionately on refinancing.

The main public dataset for this design is HMDA (Home Mortgage Disclosure Act) loan-level data. HMDA records loan purpose (purchase versus refinance), application outcome, loan amount, borrower income and race, lender identity, and — since the 2017 redesign — total borrower-paid loan costs. Those total costs are not itemized, but they include title-insurance fees along with other borrower-paid closing costs. By mapping actual state title-premium schedules onto HMDA loan amounts and locations, a researcher can construct a predicted title-cost measure and test whether it explains variation in refinance responsiveness, loan costs, and application volumes.

Two supplementary findings would strengthen the story. First, cost pass-through: if the predicted title premium explains a meaningful share of variation in HMDA total loan costs, that validates the treatment measure. Second, heterogeneity by borrower type: because title premiums are a fixed or scale-insensitive cost relative to loan size, the burden is regressive. A $900 refinance title premium represents a much larger fraction of the savings from a $100,000 loan than from a $400,000 loan. Fannie Mae's closing-cost research documents exactly this pattern: closing costs are regressive relative to sale price, and lender title insurance is one of the standard components. The effect on refinancing should therefore be strongest for smaller-balance, lower-income, and first-time buyers.

## The Data Landscape and Its Limits

The most significant obstacle to clean empirical analysis of title insurance is not methodological — it is a data gap that Treasury and NAIC have both explicitly identified.

**HMDA** provides the best publicly available mortgage data. It covers applications and originations across the country, identifies loan purpose, and after 2017 includes total borrower-paid loan costs. But it does not itemize those costs. There is no HMDA field for "title insurance premium paid." That means researchers must construct a predicted title cost from external schedules and merge it to loan records.

**NSMO** (the National Survey of Mortgage Originations) adds a borrower-experience dimension: it asks how much money borrowers needed at closing, how many lenders they considered, how they paid closing costs, and — notably — how well they understand the difference between lender's and owner's title insurance. NSMO is smaller than HMDA and not suitable for fine-grained geographic designs, but it is excellent for testing mechanisms.

**GSE loan-performance data** from Freddie Mac and Fannie Mae cover tens of millions of loans and record prepayment — that is, early payoff, which is how refinancing appears in performance data. If high-title-cost states exhibit lower prepayment hazards when rates fall, conditional on loan and borrower characteristics, that would connect the application-side HMDA evidence to realized refinancing behavior.

**Zillow ZTRAX and Redfin** data provide property-transaction and housing-market context. State title-premium schedules — available from state regulators for promulgated-rate states, or from NAIC's 2025 regulatory survey for all states — provide the treatment measure.

What is not publicly available is **line-item Closing Disclosure data**. The Uniform Closing Dataset transmits itemized Closing Disclosure information to Fannie and Freddie at loan delivery, but no public-use version of that data has been released. Fannie Mae's internal closing-cost research uses UCD data; independent researchers generally cannot. Treasury and NAIC both noted in 2024 and 2025 that this gap limits the quality of independent research, and that better public disclosure of title-cost data would be a meaningful step.

Similarly, **title claims, litigation rates, and time-to-close data** are fragmented across state courts, county records, and private insurers. The industry argues that claims data would support the prevention narrative; independent researchers cannot easily verify that argument from public records. NAIC's 2024 research request identified this as a priority gap.

## Policy Implications

The findings of a well-executed empirical study in this area would have policy implications regardless of which direction the evidence points.

If higher title-insurance costs meaningfully reduce refinancing when rates fall, the policy case for reform is stronger than commonly appreciated. Title insurance would not just be a fairness concern — it would be an obstacle to the efficient transmission of monetary policy to households. When the Federal Reserve lowers interest rates, the transmission to household cash flows depends partly on how many homeowners can afford to refinance. A structural friction that depresses refinancing in high-title-cost states slows that transmission and keeps monthly payments higher than they need to be. That finding would strengthen the case for: standardized reissue-rate discounts across all states; streamlined title products for refinance transactions; broader acceptance of attorney title opinion letters as alternatives to full policies; expanded use of Iowa-style flat-fee or public-option models; and GSE reform that allows more cost-efficient title protection in secondary-market eligible loans.

If instead the evidence shows little effect on refinancing despite substantial title-cost variation, the interpretation cuts the other way. It would imply either that title costs are second-order relative to other refinance frictions (rate lock, appraisal costs, servicer inertia, information barriers), or that the protection and standardization value of the existing system is sufficient to justify the cost at the margin consumers actually face. That finding would support more modest reforms centered on transparency — better itemization on Closing Disclosures, clearer disclosure of reissue discounts, improved consumer shopping tools — rather than structural overhaul.

Either result advances the policy debate. What the field currently lacks is the rigorous empirical evidence needed to distinguish between those two scenarios.

---

## Conclusion

Title insurance occupies an unusual position in American housing finance. It is mandatory for virtually every mortgaged purchase and refinance, but consumers have little ability to shop for it. Its premiums are set by state schedules that vary widely, but the reasons for that variation are rarely explained to borrowers. Its loss ratios are among the lowest in any insurance market, but the industry argues — with some validity — that the real work happens before the closing, not after.

The policy community is paying closer attention than it has in decades. Treasury, CFPB, and NAIC have all signaled concern and called for better evidence. The academic literature has documented the market's structural oddities but has not yet produced a clean causal estimate of how title costs affect mortgage-market behavior.

The most tractable and policy-relevant estimate is one that the existing public data can credibly support: how much do required title-insurance costs dampen mortgage refinancing when interest rates decline? The answer would illuminate not just the title-insurance market but a larger question about which friction costs in the mortgage system matter most for household financial health and the effective reach of monetary policy.

The data are available, the design is feasible, and the policy moment is right. The research is overdue.
